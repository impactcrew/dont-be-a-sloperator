# Kill List

These phrases and patterns are banned. If you catch yourself writing any of them, delete and rewrite.

## Hollow Verb Phrases

Verbs that sound active but say nothing about what the thing actually does.

- "built to understand"
- "designed to empower"
- "crafted to deliver"
- "engineered to perform"
- "created to inspire"
- "made to transform"
- "developed to enhance"

The fix: replace with what the thing literally does. "Built to understand your needs" becomes "reads your past orders and suggests sizes."

## Filler Words

Words that pad sentences without adding meaning. Delete them and the sentence gets better.

- seamlessly
- leverage
- elevate
- unlock
- reimagine
- holistic
- robust
- cutting-edge
- innovative
- next-generation
- best-in-class
- world-class
- synergy
- streamline
- optimize (when used vaguely)
- empower
- revolutionize
- disrupt

## Buzzword Stacks

Multiple filler words chained together, pretending to be a value proposition.

- "AI-powered solution built to engage"
- "data-driven platform designed to transform"
- "cloud-native infrastructure built for scale"
- "end-to-end solution that drives results"

The fix: name the specific capability, the specific user, and the specific outcome.

## Formulaic Rhythm Tricks

Sentence structures that AI defaults to because they sound punchy but contain no information.

- "No X. No Y. Just Z." (e.g., "No hassle. No complexity. Just results.")
- "X meets Y." (e.g., "Innovation meets simplicity.")
- "Where X meets Y." (e.g., "Where technology meets humanity.")
- "More than just X." (e.g., "More than just a platform.")
- "X, redefined." (e.g., "Customer service, redefined.")
- "The future of X." (e.g., "The future of work.")

## Anthropomorphizing Abstractions

Giving human qualities to things that don't have them.

- "a crowd that feels every lap"
- "technology that cares"
- "a platform that listens"
- "data that tells a story"
- "AI that understands you"
- "a community that thrives"

The fix: describe what actually happens. "A platform that listens" might mean "users submit feature requests and vote on them." Say that instead.

## The Generic Test

After writing anything, apply this test:

1. Replace your product/company/client name with a competitor's name
2. Does the copy still make sense?
3. If yes, it's too generic. Rewrite until swapping names would break it.
